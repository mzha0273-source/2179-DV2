Victoria’s international education recovery is strong — but uneven

From 2019 to 2025, Victoria’s international education sector moved through a sharp contraction, a rapid rebound, and a more selective 2025 market. This page follows the recovery through enrolments, commencements, export value, student visas, source countries, sectors and state share.

01 · The recovery pattern

Recovery did not happen at the same speed for every indicator

Export value reached a new high in 2025, while commencements and visa lodgements show a more cautious pipeline. The contrast matters because total enrolments include continuing students, while commencements and visas are earlier signals of future demand.

Export value is above 2019, but commencements and visa lodgements are still weaker signals.

Victorian enrolments peaked at 328,000 in 2024 and eased to 322,000 in 2025.

Commencements fell from 172,000 in 2024 to 147,000 in 2025.

The high 2025 export value sits beside slightly lower enrolments, suggesting value recovered faster than volume.

02 · Where demand sits

The recovery is concentrated by state, sector and source market

Victoria remains the second-largest Australian state for international enrolment market share. Within Victoria, higher education and VET dominate the sector mix, and India plus China account for the largest source-country volumes.

Higher education is the largest sector, with 52% of Victorian international enrolments.

NSW and Victoria together account for most of the national enrolment market share.

India and China form the two largest source markets for Victoria in 2025.

Nepal and Indonesia moved up the ranking, while Colombia and the Philippines moved down.

03 · Signals for the next cycle

Early indicators point to a tighter, more competitive market

The February visa-lodgement series does not measure enrolments directly, but it is useful as an early signal. It shows a strong post-border-reopening rebound in 2023 followed by a lower level in 2025 and 2026.

February YTD offshore primary visa lodgements recovered slightly in 2026 after the 2025 low.

The heatmap separates high-value recovery from softer new-student indicators.

The 2025 sector shares imply about 167,000 higher education enrolments and 113,000 VET enrolments.

The top two nationalities contribute more than half of the top-10 source-market total.

Overall message

Victoria’s international education sector has recovered strongly in export value and remains nationally important. However, the pipeline indicators suggest the post-pandemic rebound is no longer simply about growth everywhere: future strength depends on balancing state competition, sector mix and source-market concentration.
